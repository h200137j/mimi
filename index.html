Lunavere

They say the space between stars
is not emptiness, but a language—
a braille of quasars spelling want,
a syntax of event horizons
where light bends into prayer.
I have mapped this silence,
Michelle—
traced your name in the static
of dying constellations,
in the afterglow of supernovae
that took millennia to say you.

The astronomers never warned me
how a heart could orbit a singularity,
how absence becomes its own dark matter—
a gravity so dense it invents new alphabets:
saudade in the ache of my ribs,
tokka humming through unheld hands,
all my lexicons collapsing
into the event horizon of your hair.

But tonight, the void hums differently.
A neutrino pierces the black—
your laughter, decoded
from gamma rays to skin.
I am Pythagoras recalculating the spheres,
finding your pulse in the Fibonacci spiral
of galaxies I once called distance.

They will write psalms of how we warped time—
two bodies refusing entropy,
a love louder than the birth-cry
of any star. Let them chart
the redshift of my breath
when your voice says soon,
let them parse the mathematics
of my palms relearning
the topography of your face.

When at last the cosmos grows small
as a held breath, I will not speak
of light-years or infinities.
I will simply kneel in the wreckage
of all my invented words,
and whisper,
Behold—
the lunavere
is Michelle.

The Fates once wove our thread with venom—
spun it taut as the bowstring of Artemis,
then severed it clean, a sickle's kiss.
Now I drift where even Styx grows jealous,
Michelle,
a shade without his Eurydice,
cursing the oars of Charon's leaky boat
as he ferries my pulse to its cold eclipse.

They say Sisyphus invented longing,
his stone a monument to almost.
I've rolled a boulder of syllables up Olympus,
each one crumbling to ash before your name.
I carved your face into Athena's shield—
the Gorgon's stare turned soft, unstoned.
I bargained with Hermes' lyre:
Trade my voice for a single sigh of hers.
The gods only laughed,
their nectar sour with pity.

But tonight, the Erotes stir.
A dove, fat with ambrosia,
drops a feather in my palm—
your letter, inked in ichor and fig.
Soon, it hums, soon.
I scale the ribs of Ithaca's cliffs,
no longer Odysseus, but a man
who'd burn every fleet
to warm his hands at your hearth.

Let poets sing of Achilles' rage,
of Helen's face launching hollow ships.
I am Orpheus, undone but unashamed,
relearning the anthem of your pulse—
a hymn that melts the wax in Icarus' wings,
turns Lethe's draught to spring rain.
When you arrive, the Underworld
will forget its own name.

Do you see?
Cerberus lies docile at your step.
Persephone spills her pomegranate seeds
to crown your hair.
The stars? They're just old gods
peeling back the sky to watch you pass.

Do not speak of Elysium.
I've knelt in temples, breathed their dust,
and found no prayer brighter
than the hitch in your breath
when you say here, I'm here.
The Pantheon's a ruin.
The true myth is the way
your fingers rewrite my spine
into a spine of living marble—
not a relic, but a colonnade
where dawn climbs, hungry and new.

When you come, I'll snap the Fates' shears
with my bare hands.
No more odysseies, no trials,
just the honeyed arithmetic
of your laugh in my throat.
Let the heavens rot.
We'll build our own legends
from the rubble of theirs.

Behold—
the lunavere
is Michelle.
